Fluorescence Microscopy and Image Analysis

BME Measurement and Analysis Laboratory (14:125:315)

For this laboratory project in BME Measurement and Analysis Laboratory (14:125:315), we explored fluorescence microscopy as a quantitative tool for biomedical image capture and analysis. Fluorescence imaging was used to evaluate signal-to-noise ratio under varying exposure times and imaging conditions.

We performed live/dead cell viability analysis and quantified cell populations using ImageJ and MATLAB. In addition, Fluorescence Recovery After Photobleaching (FRAP) experiments were conducted to analyze molecular diffusion and estimate the viscosity of protein-dense droplets.

This project demonstrates the application of quantitative fluorescence techniques for biological characterization and highlights the integration of experimental imaging with computational analysis.

Live/Dead Cell Viability Analysis: This figure illustrates the comparison between raw fluorescence microscopy images and post-processed segmentation using ImageJ and MATLAB for live/dead cell analysis. Fluorescent filters were used to selectively visualize viable and non-viable cells, after which image thresholding and watershed algorithms enabled quantitative cell counting and signal-to-noise evaluation. This workflow demonstrates how experimental fluorescence imaging can be integrated with computational analysis to extract biologically meaningful metrics.

Fluorescence Recovery After Photobleaching (FRAP) Model: This figure shows the fluorescence recovery curve obtained following photobleaching of a defined region, along with an exponential fit generated in MATLAB. The recovery kinetics were used to estimate molecular diffusion properties and infer the viscosity of protein-dense droplets. This analysis highlights the application of dynamic fluorescence techniques for probing molecular transport in biological systems.
